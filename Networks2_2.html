Networks II: Page 2 - UDP: Speed, Efficiency, and Real-Time Communication

The Need for Speed

In our previous page, we saw how TCP provides a reliable, ordered, and error-checked "conversation" at the cost of speed and overhead. But what if you are streaming a live video? If a single frame gets lost, waiting for a retransmission would cause the video to buffer and freeze. You would rather see a minor glitch than have the video stop entirely. This is where UDP (User Datagram Protocol) shines. UDP is a connectionless protocol designed for speed and simplicity. It sacrifices the reliability features of TCP to reduce latency, making it the backbone of real-time communication.

1. Connectionless: Just Send It

Unlike TCP, UDP does not perform a three-way handshake. It doesn't track sequence numbers, and it doesn't acknowledge received packets. It simply takes the application data, wraps it in a minimal header, and "fires" it at the destination IP address. If the packet arrives, great! If it's lost in the network, the sender never knows and doesn't care.

The Minimal UDP Header (8 bytes)


| Source Port (16 bits) | Destination Port (16 bits) |
| Length (16 bits)      | Checksum (16 bits)         |
| Data (Payload)                                     |

Compare this to the complex, 20-60 byte TCP header, and you can see why UDP is so much faster—it has significantly less "baggage" attached to every packet.

2. When Should You Use UDP?

UDP is perfect for scenarios where "some data loss is acceptable" and "delay is unacceptable."

  • Voice over IP (VoIP): A dropped audio packet just causes a tiny click, not a pause.
  • Video Streaming: Dropped video frames are better than a buffering screen.
  • Online Gaming: You need the *latest* player position immediately, not an old position that arrived late due to TCP retransmission.
  • DNS Lookups: DNS queries are small, and it's faster to just re-send the request than to establish a full TCP connection.

Real-World Relationship: The Postcard vs. The Registered Letter

Think of TCP like a Registered Letter. You write a letter, and the post office gives you a tracking number. They guarantee it will arrive. When the recipient gets the letter, they must sign for it (Acknowledgment). If the letter doesn't arrive in three days, you get a notification that the delivery failed and you send a second copy. Think of UDP like Sending a Bunch of Postcards. You drop 100 postcards in the mailbox. Some might get there before others, some might be delivered in reverse order, and some might fall out of the mail truck and disappear forever. You don't get tracking numbers, but the postcards were much faster and cheaper to send. UDP is the "Postcard" protocol—perfect for fast-moving data that doesn't need a signature.

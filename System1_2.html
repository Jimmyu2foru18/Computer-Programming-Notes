System Design I: Page 2 - Requirements & Capacity

Defining the Goal

Before you draw a single box on a whiteboard, you must understand what you are building and how big it needs to be. This is the Requirements Gathering and Capacity Estimation phase. If you skip this, you are building in the dark. You risk over-engineering (wasting money on a system 100x larger than necessary) or under-engineering (having your system crash the moment it goes live).

1. Functional vs. Non-Functional Requirements

  • Functional Requirements: What the system does. Example: "Users must be able to upload profile pictures" or "The system must support real-time messaging."
  • Non-Functional Requirements (Quality Attributes): How the system behaves. Example: "The system must support 10,000 concurrent users with less than 200ms latency" or "The system must be 99.99% available."

2. Back-of-the-Envelope Capacity Estimation

You don't need a supercomputer to estimate capacity; you need basic arithmetic. You should always calculate:

  • QPS (Queries Per Second): How many requests will hit your API during peak times?
  • Storage: How much data will you accumulate in a year?
  • Bandwidth: How much data will be moving in and out of your servers?

# Basic QPS Calculation
daily_users = 1000000
req_per_user = 10
seconds_in_day = 86400

avg_qps = (daily_users * req_per_user) / seconds_in_day
peak_qps = avg_qps * 3 # Assume peak traffic is 3x avg

Real-World Relationship: The Architect and the Client

Think of Requirements Gathering like an Architect meeting a client. If the client says "build me a house," the architect asks "how many people will live there?" (Capacity Estimation), "do you need a garage?" (Functional Req), and "should it be eco-friendly?" (Non-Functional Req). If the architect skips these questions and just starts building, they might build a single-story cottage when the client needed a two-story mansion. You must understand the constraints (budget, timeline, scale) to design the right system.

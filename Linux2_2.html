Linux II: Page 2 - Process Management

The Life of a Process

In Linux, everything that is running is a Process. From the GUI desktop environment to the background services handling your web traffic, each process has a unique PID (Process ID). Managing these processes—monitoring their CPU and memory usage, troubleshooting stalled or crashing programs, and terminating runaway processes—is a daily task for any Linux administrator. Improper process management can lead to system instability, so knowing how to control these tasks safely is vital.

1. Monitoring Processes

You can't manage what you can't see. Several tools allow you to peek into the system's activity:

  • ps: Gives you a static snapshot of processes. Use ps aux for a comprehensive look.
  • top: Provides a real-time, dynamic view of processes, sorted by resource usage.
  • htop: An interactive, color-coded, and much more user-friendly alternative to top (you might need to install it).

# View all processes
ps aux
# Real-time monitoring
top

2. Controlling Processes (Signals)

Linux manages processes by sending them Signals. The most important signals are:

  • SIGTERM (15): The polite request: "Please finish what you are doing and shut down gracefully."
  • SIGKILL (9): The forced shutdown: "Stop immediately! No grace period." Use this only when a process is unresponsive.

# Gracefully terminate
kill PID
# Forcefully kill
kill -9 PID

Real-World Relationship: The Office Manager

Think of Process Management like an Office Manager. They have a list (ps) of all employees and what they are working on. When the office gets busy, they look at a real-time monitor (top) to see who is overloaded. If an employee (a process) is crashing and making a mess, the manager first asks them to clean up and go home (SIGTERM). If they refuse to leave and continue making a mess, the manager has to call security to escort them out immediately (SIGKILL).

Software Engineering I: Page 10 - Patterns in Practice: Architectural Strategy

Beyond the Catalog

In the previous three pages, we explored the "Gang of Four" design patterns—Creational, Structural, and Behavioral. However, being a great software engineer isn't just about memorizing these patterns; it's about knowing when to use them and, more importantly, when not to. Design patterns are tools, not goals. A common mistake for junior developers is "Pattern Overkill," where they try to force every problem into a complex pattern, resulting in over-engineered, unreadable code. Professional engineering is the art of balancing flexibility with simplicity.

1. The Decision Matrix: Choosing a Pattern

How do you know which pattern to reach for? You must first identify the Axis of Change. What part of your system is likely to change or grow in the future?

  • If the variety of objects will grow: Use a Factory or Builder.
  • If you need to connect legacy code: Use an Adapter or Facade.
  • If you need to add features dynamically: Use a Decorator.
  • If you need to react to events: Use an Observer.
  • If the business rules/algorithms will change: Use a Strategy.

2. Patterns in the Wild

You are already using design patterns every day, even if you don't realize it. Modern frameworks are built entirely on these concepts:

  • Spring Framework (Java): Uses the Singleton pattern for its Beans and Dependency Injection (a form of Strategy/Factory) to manage objects.
  • React (JavaScript): Uses the Observer pattern to update the UI when state changes.
  • Django (Python): Uses the Facade pattern to simplify complex database queries into simple model methods.

3. The Dangers of Over-Engineering

Following the KISS and YAGNI principles is crucial when applying patterns. If you only have two types of payments, a simple if-else is perfectly fine. Don't build a complex "PaymentStrategy" system until you actually have enough complexity to justify it. Every pattern adds a layer of Indirection, which makes the code harder to follow for someone who just wants to see the main logic.

Real-World Relationship: The Toolbelt and the Over-Built Bridge

Think of Design Patterns like a Master Carpenter's Toolbelt. A beginner only has a hammer and sees every problem as a nail. A master has 50 specialized tools. They don't use all 50 on every job; they know exactly when to pull out the "dovetail saw" and when to just use a "standard screwdriver." Think of Over-Engineering like building a Golden Bridge to cross a 2-foot wide stream. It's beautiful, it follows all the rules of engineering, and it will last 1,000 years—but it was a waste of time and money when a simple wooden plank (Clean, Simple Code) would have worked just as well. Use the pattern only when the "stream" of your data is too deep for a plank.

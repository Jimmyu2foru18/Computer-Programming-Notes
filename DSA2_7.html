DSA II: Page 7 - Graphs: Introduction

Mapping Relationships

While Trees model 1-to-many hierarchical relationships, Graphs are far more powerful: they model many-to-many relationships. A graph is a collection of Vertices (or nodes) connected by Edges. Graphs are how we model social networks (users are vertices, friendships are edges), road maps (intersections are vertices, roads are edges), and the entire structure of the World Wide Web (web pages are vertices, hyperlinks are edges).

1. Graph Types & Properties

  • Directed: Edges have a specific direction (A → B means you can only go from A to B).
  • Undirected: Edges have no direction (A — B means you can go both ways).
  • Weighted: Edges have a cost/weight associated with them (e.g., travel time between two cities).
  • Unweighted: All edges are treated equally.

2. Representing Graphs

How do you store a graph in a computer? Two main ways:

  • Adjacency Matrix: A 2D array (V x V). matrix[i][j] = 1 if there's an edge between vertex i and j, 0 otherwise. Best for dense graphs.
  • Adjacency List: An array of lists. Each vertex has a list of its neighbors. Best for sparse graphs (most real-world graphs).

Real-World Relationship: The City Subway System

Think of a Graph like a City Subway Map. Each subway station is a Vertex. The tracks connecting the stations are the Edges. If the train only runs in one direction between stations, it's a Directed Graph. If the train runs back and forth, it's Undirected. The time it takes to travel between stations is the Weight. This map (graph) is essential for subway operators to manage traffic and for commuters to calculate the fastest route to get to work.

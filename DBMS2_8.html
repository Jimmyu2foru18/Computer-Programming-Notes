DBMS II: Page 8 - Business Intelligence: Data Warehousing & OLAP

Analyzing the Past to Predict the Future

Most of the databases we've studied so far are **OLTP (Online Transactional Processing)** systems. They are designed for fast, frequent updates (like a customer buying a shirt). However, large corporations also need **OLAP (Online Analytical Processing)**. They need to analyze 10 years of sales data to find trends. Running these massive, complex queries on a live transactional database would slow down the store for everyone. To solve this, engineers build a **Data Warehouse**—a central repository of integrated data from one or more disparate sources. Data warehouses are optimized for reading and analysis rather than writing.

1. Dimensional Modeling: Star & Snowflake Schemas

In a Data Warehouse, we don't use standard normalization. Instead, we use **Dimensional Modeling** to make queries faster and easier to understand for business analysts.

  • Fact Table: The center of the model. It contains quantitative measurements (e.g., total sales amount, quantity sold) and foreign keys to dimensions.
  • Dimension Tables: The "points" of the star. They contain descriptive context (e.g., Product name, Store location, Date details).

A Star Schema is the simplest form, where fact tables connect directly to dimensions. A Snowflake Schema is more normalized, where dimensions themselves are broken down into sub-dimensions (e.g., Product points to Category, which points to Department).

2. OLAP Operations: Slicing and Dicing

Analysts use specialized operations to explore the "Data Cube":

  1. Roll-up: Summarizing data to a higher level (e.g., viewing sales by Year instead of by Month).
  2. Drill-down: Moving from a summary to more detail (e.g., viewing sales for a specific Store after looking at the whole Region).
  3. Slice: Picking a single dimension for a view (e.g., "All sales in 2023").
  4. Dice: Picking a specific sub-cube (e.g., "Sales of Shoes in New York during January").

Real-World Relationship: The Cash Register and the Corporate Report

Think of an OLTP Database like a Cash Register. It needs to be incredibly fast and reliable for every single transaction. It doesn't care about what happened last year; it only cares about the customer standing in front of it right now. Think of a Data Warehouse (OLAP) like the CEO's Year-End Report. The CEO doesn't want to see every single receipt from every store. They want to see a chart showing "Revenue by Region" or "Profit by Product Category." The Data Warehouse takes all the messy register receipts, cleans them up, and organizes them so the CEO can "Drill-down" into why one specific region is underperforming.

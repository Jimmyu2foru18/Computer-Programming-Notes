System Design I: Page 1 - Intro & Principles

The Architect's Mindset

System Design is the process of defining the architecture, modules, interfaces, and data for a system to satisfy specified requirements. It is not just about writing code; it's about making high-level decisions regarding how that code will perform, scale, and remain secure when thousands or millions of users interact with it simultaneously. A good system designer balances trade-offs—such as choosing between speed and consistency, or between complexity and maintainability—to arrive at an optimal solution for the business problem at hand.

1. The Fundamental Principles

  • Scalability: The ability of a system to handle increased load, either by adding more resources (Horizontal/Vertical scaling).
  • Reliability: The ability of the system to continue working, even in the face of failures, through redundancy and failover mechanisms.
  • Availability: The percentage of time a system is operational and accessible to users (often measured in "nines," e.g., 99.999% "five nines").

Real-World Relationship: The City Planner

Think of System Design like a City Planner. They don't just build a house; they plan the roads, the water supply, the power grid, and the zoning laws for thousands of people. If they plan for a small town but the city grows rapidly, the roads will clog (Performance Bottlenecks) and the power grid will fail (System Outage). A good city planner anticipates growth and designs the infrastructure—the bridges, the highways, the backup power plants—so the city remains functional as it expands.

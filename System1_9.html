System Design I: Page 9 - Arch Patterns: High-Level

The Foundation of Scalability

Before you implement a single feature, you must choose the right Architectural Pattern. This high-level structural decision defines how your system components are organized, how they communicate, and how they scale. A choice made here (e.g., Monolithic vs. Microservices) will be incredibly difficult—and expensive—to change later.

1. Major Architecture Patterns

  • Monolithic: The entire application is a single, self-contained unit. Simple to build and deploy for startups, but becomes difficult to scale and maintain as the app grows.
  • Microservices: Decomposes the application into small, independent services, each handling a specific business function (e.g., User Service, Order Service). Highly scalable, but introduces massive operational complexity (inter-service communication, distributed consistency).
  • Serverless: You deploy individual functions that trigger on events (e.g., an AWS Lambda function running when an image is uploaded). You don't manage servers, just code. Extremely cost-effective for event-driven tasks, but can have "cold start" latency issues.

2. Choosing the Pattern

There is no "best" pattern, only the pattern that best fits your requirements and team structure. Startups usually start Monolithic to move fast, then migrate parts of the application to Microservices only when scale necessitates it.

Real-World Relationship: The Business Structure

Think of a Monolithic architecture like a Small Business where everyone wears every hat. The accountant also does marketing, and the manager cleans the office. It works fine when there are 3 employees, but it's chaotic when there are 300. Think of Microservices like a Large Corporation with distinct departments (HR, Engineering, Sales). Each department is independent, handles its own hiring and budget, and they only interact through formal company procedures. It's much more scalable, but requires a complex management system to coordinate them.
